# Patterns: ai-commentary

**Last updated:** 2026-04-02 — 3 scripts analyzed

---

## Hook Patterns
Three distinct hook types observed: bold absurd claim ("This dog thinks he had superpowers"), unexpected discovery interrupting a mundane action ("he was shocked to find..."), and fable-reframe ("This is why the intelligent turtle will always beat the quick rabbit"). All three open with a single declarative sentence. Two of three use a reaction word ("shocked") or front-load the conclusion ("This is why...") to create immediate forward pull. Hooks promise either an explanation or a reveal — never just a topic.

## Pacing Patterns
Pacing adapts to emotional register. Prank/comedic scripts use rapid staccato escalation (one beat per sentence, fast turns). Emotional/nurturing content moves slowly with time-passage language ("Slowly," "before long"). Explanatory/fable content uses steady chronological stepping. All three approaches withhold the resolution until the final sentence.

## Sentence Structure Norms
Short declarative sentences are the default. "But" is the most consistent structural connector across all scripts — used as a pivot, contrast marker, and escalation signal. Compound connectors ("Because," "And while," "Even though") appear in longer emotional or explanatory beats. Average sentence length stays short regardless of tone.

## Storytelling Approaches
All three scripts add creative framing not visible in the raw footage — attributed emotions, fictional backstory (hunted mother), or fable allegory — to give the video a narrative logic it wouldn't have on its own. Subject emotional state is tracked throughout every script, even when the subject is an animal. Endings resolve both the narrative event and an emotional or thematic thread simultaneously.

## What Consistently Works
Hooks that promise either a surprising explanation or a reveal outperform descriptive openers. Added fictional or allegorical framing converts raw footage into a story with stakes. Tracking the subject's emotional journey makes viewers invest in the outcome. One-sentence endings that close both the plot and the emotional arc land the hardest. The word "But" consistently signals turning points and primes the viewer for a shift.
